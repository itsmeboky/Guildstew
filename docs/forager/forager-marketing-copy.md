## The Manifesto (for landing page or Kickstarter intro)

Every VTT on the market freezes when you stop watching. The torches don't burn down. The shopkeeper doesn't go home. The wolves don't get hungry. Your "world" is a static image with miniatures dragged across it, and the second the session ends, time stops.

That's not a world. That's a screensaver.

We built Forager because we got tired of pretending the map was alive when it wasn't. Forager is the first TTRPG companion that actually *simulates the world your party is exploring.* The baker has a schedule and follows it. The town watch patrols on a route. The bandits camped in the forest stay camped — or move — or attack a passing caravan — whether or not anyone is looking. You paint a place, and it becomes a place that exists.

When you hit play, the world starts living. When you hit pause, it stops. When you fast-forward a week of game time because the party went to sea, you come back to a village that has had a week. The innkeeper has aged. The smuggler the party crossed has hired muscle. The crops are different. The map *remembers.*

This is the thing nobody else is shipping. Roll20 is a battle map. Foundry is a fancier battle map. Inkarnate is an art tool. Owlbear Rodeo is a token mover. None of them simulate. Not one.

Forager does. And it runs on top of Guildstew, the platform thousands of GMs already use to manage their campaigns. The maps you make in Forager talk to your character sheets, your combat engine, your homebrew rules, your marketplace. It's not a separate app. It's the spatial layer your campaign was missing.

Welcome to a living world.

## Feature Spotlights

*Each one written punchy. Pull whichever lands hardest for your audience.*

### The Living Simulation

> **Your baker bakes. Your guards patrol. Your villains plot.**
> Every NPC in Forager has a schedule. They wake up. They go to work. They go home. They argue with their spouses. They forget to lock the back door on Tuesdays.
> Hit play. Watch it happen. Hit fast-forward. Watch a day pass in two minutes — and the world is exactly where it should be when the clock stops.

### Auto Fog of War

> **You will never paint another fog of war polygon.**
> Drop a tree, it casts a shadow. Drop a wall, it cuts the line of sight. The fog of war is *automatic.* The visibility polygon updates in real time as your players walk through the world. No setup. No masking. No fiddly Roll20 polygon-clicking at 11pm on a Thursday because someone moved.
> This is the feature you've been begging for since 2019.

### Terrain That Matters

> **Water slows you. Mud leaves tracks. Tall grass hides you.**
> In Forager, terrain isn't paint. It's *physics.* Wade through a river and your speed drops. Sprint across a mire and you leave footprints a tracker can read three game hours later. Crouch in the brambleberry bushes and the bandits walk past without seeing you.
> Every tile has rules. The party will learn them the hard way.

### The Hybrid Building Model

> **Drop a tavern. Or build one stone by stone. Or both.**
> Need a town fast? Slap down prefab buildings — tavern, smithy, butcher, inn — each with a name, a description, a doorway. Done. Five minutes, town's full.
> Need the dungeon's throne room to count? Switch to the Room Maker, paint the floor, place the throne, set the torches, write the lore. Walk in and the camera centers, the roof lifts away, the chamber breathes.
> Most buildings get the quick treatment. The important ones get the artist's eye. You choose, per building.

### Stealth That Makes Sense

> **Hide in a bush. It works because it should.**
> No skill check. No die roll. Step into the bushes, the screen tells you HIDDEN, and the hostile patrolling the corridor walks past two feet away. Two seconds of cover and even a creature actively pursuing you loses your trail.
> This isn't a Roll20 token tooltip. It's terrain doing what terrain does. Hide where you can hide. Run where you can run.

### Day, Night, and the In-Between

> **Hit night. Watch the village go to sleep.**
> A real day-night cycle. The baker closes up at six. The watchman starts his rounds at sundown. The shadows lengthen. The light goes cool and silver. Your players are crossing a square they remember from morning and it's an entirely different place at midnight.
> Light a torch. Watch its glow puddle on the cobblestones. Step into the moonlight. Wonder what's just out of sight.

### Walls That Actually Stop You

> **You cannot walk through a wall. Imagine.**
> A revolutionary new feature in TTRPG software: walls that work like walls. Doors that work like doors. You enter buildings through entrances. You don't phase through stone. The architecture is real.
> We can't believe we have to put this on a feature list, but here we are.

### Combat That Hands Off Cleanly

> **The map runs the world. The engine you trust runs the fight.**
> When swords come out, Forager hands off seamlessly to Guildstew's Combat Engine v2 — the same combat system you already use, with all your house rules, all your homebrew, all your character data. Initiative rolls. Attack of opportunity. Death saves. The works.
> When the dust settles, control returns to the map. The simulation resumes. The world keeps living.

### A World That Remembers

> **Burn down the inn. It stays burned.**
> Forager doesn't reset between sessions. Every meaningful thing the party does *persists.* You killed the smuggler in chapter two? He's not in chapter four. You stole from the shop in the spring? The shopkeeper still has the disposition shift in the fall.
> Maps are save files in Forager. They grow as your campaign grows. Sessions become geology.

### Fast-Forward the World

> **The party is at sea for a week. Hit a button. Skip to landfall.**
> A full game day passes in two minutes of real time. Two weeks pass in twenty minutes. And every minute of game time, the NPCs you've placed are following their schedules, the dispositions are shifting, the encounter zones are evaluating. When the party comes back, the world has moved on without them.
> This is the feature that sells the simulation. This is the moment people *get it.*

### Maps as Marketplace

> **Make dungeons. Sell dungeons. Buy dungeons.**
> Forager maps publish directly to the Brewery (free) or the Tavern (paid). Authored a haunted island? List it. Generated a cohesive city with NPCs and lore? List it. Want to buy something? Browse the catalog and drop a whole campaign's worth of geography into your table.
> Spatial content is content. Now it's tradable.

### Procgen With a Brain

> **Generate a haunted port town in thirty seconds.**
> AI procgen built on the same Edge Function infrastructure that powers Guildstew's character tools. Type a prompt — "fortified mining town in a snow biome with a heretical undercurrent" — and Forager generates the buildings, the paths, the residents, the schedules, the lore blurbs. All consistent. All editable.
> Not a roll-on-a-table generator. A coherent world-builder. Edit it, tweak it, ship it.

### The Inspector

> **Click anything. See everything.**
> Click an NPC: see their full schedule, current goal, disposition, and recent events. Click a building: see who's inside, what's stored, what's been written. Click a tile: see what's painted, what blocks sight, what slows movement. *Nothing in Forager is opaque.*
> A complex simulation only feels deep if it's legible. We made it legible.

### Encounter Zones

> **Set the trap. Walk away. Wait.**
> Paint an invisible zone over the bandit camp. Set the trigger: *if party enters between 9pm and 4am AND the bounty quest is active, three bandits attack.* You're done. The trap will spring itself when the party walks into it, even if you forgot about it.
> Game-running is half stage management. Forager runs the stage for you.

### Multiple Scenes That All Live

> **Overworld. Dungeon. Ship. Dreams. All running at once.**
> You can have any number of maps active in a campaign — a town, a wilderness region, a tomb beneath the town, the deck of a ship anchored offshore. Each one ticks. Each one simulates. The dungeon you placed bandits in three sessions ago is *still there,* and they're *still patrolling,* and they're *still going to be there* when the party finally returns.

### The Room Maker and the Community Library

> **Author a tavern. Publish it. Watch other GMs use it.**
> Spent three hours designing the perfect dwarven longhouse with eighteen pieces of furniture and a smoke-cured ham hanging from the rafters? Save it. Publish it as a Brew. Other Guildstew GMs download it and drop it into their towns.
> Cartographers and dungeon designers are a totally different talent pool from rules-tinkerers. Forager brings them into your creator economy.

### Footprints, Tracks, and Trails

> **Run through a puddle. Leave a trail.**
> Walk through water — wet footprints behind you, fading over time. Walk through mud — tracks the bandit huntress can read. Walk through snow — a path back to your camp.
> Tiny touches. Massive immersion.

### Living Light

> **Place a torch. Watch it flicker.**
> Lanterns. Campfires. Candles. Magical glow stones. Every light source casts a warm pool of light into the dark, flickers like real fire, and gets blown out by rain. Plan dungeon torches. Strategize about lantern oil. Realize you're playing a different game than you thought you were.

## "Imagine This" Scenarios (Trailer Beats / Site Hero Reel)

### Scene 1 — The Living Village

> The map opens on a quiet village at dawn. The clock reads 06:00. A single dot — the baker — leaves her cottage and walks down a dirt path. The sun rises. The clock advances. The town watch emerges from the watchhouse and starts his patrol. Two NPCs cross paths in the market and stop to talk. The party hasn't even arrived yet. The world is already happening.

### Scene 2 — Into the Dark

> The party walks east. A cave mouth in the cliffside, pulsing red. They step inside. The screen contracts. The cool moonlit world they left collapses to a torch-radius. Walls of stone slick with damp. A creature breathes somewhere in the dark. One of the players spots it — a flash of red glow at the edge of their light. The combat engine takes over.

### Scene 3 — The Sneak

> The rogue can't fight. The lurker is too strong. They duck into a moss-choked alcove. The screen lights up HIDDEN. The lurker pads past, growling. Six seconds of held breath. The rogue slips toward the treasure chamber while the lurker patrols an empty corridor.

### Scene 4 — Time Skip

> The party boards a ship. Two weeks at sea. The GM hits fast-forward. The clock spins. In the village they left behind, the watchman gets promoted. The baker hires an apprentice. A merchant arrives with news from the south. None of it is scripted. All of it is the simulation living. When the party makes landfall, the village they remember is no longer the village that's there.

### Scene 5 — The Marketplace

> A new GM, prepping their first campaign. They browse the Tavern. A free dungeon catches their eye: *"The Salt-Throated Reliquary,"* by a cartographer they've never heard of. Four chambers. Eighteen rooms. Three NPCs with full schedules and disposition systems. They click install. The maps drop into their campaign. They press play. The dungeon comes alive.
