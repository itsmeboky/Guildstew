## More Feature Spotlights

### Triggered Events — The World That Stages Itself

> **Set the trap. Walk away. Forget about it. Let it spring.**
>
> Paint an invisible zone anywhere in the world. Attach a condition: *if the party crosses this bridge at night AND the cursed amulet is in their inventory.* Attach a consequence: *summon the bridge-keeper. Trigger combat. Flag the bone-river quest as active.*
>
> You're done. You will not have to remember this trigger exists. You will not have to be at the wheel when it fires. Three sessions later, your players will walk into it because they walked into it, and the world will react because the world *was set up to react.* Forager runs the stage so you can stay in the story.
>
> Combat triggers. Plot flags. Ambient events. Conditional cascades. The author writes the rules. The simulation handles the performance.

### Interaction — The World Is Touchable

> **Look. Examine. Search. Listen. Climb. Steal. Talk.**
>
> Every object in Forager is *interactable.* The bookshelf has a description, a search check, maybe a hidden compartment if the right roll lands. The door has a listen check that reveals what's happening on the other side. The cliff has a climb check, a fall distance, and a chance of dropping your torch. The chest has a lock, a trap, contents, and the disposition shift if you got caught lifting it.
>
> No more "the GM tells you what you see." Your players *look* at things. They *examine* the contract pinned to the tavern wall. They *listen* at the door to the inquisitor's chambers. The world has *texture,* and they can run their hands across it.
>
> This is the MUD half of the heritage paying off. Every space is full of things that can be touched, named, and remembered.

### Weather — The Sky Has a Mind of Its Own

> **Rain puts out your torches. Fog hides the patrol. Snow takes prints.**
>
> Weather isn't a backdrop in Forager. It's *another system.* Rain extinguishes flame and turns dirt paths into mud that records every footfall. Fog cuts vision distance in half — even in daylight, even outside, even when nothing is supposed to be hidden. Snow leaves tracks the bandit huntress can read three game days later. Wind carries scent; a storm shakes the rafters of the abandoned tower.
>
> Run a scheduled forecast or call the weather by hand. Either way, every other system reacts. NPC schedules account for downpour — the baker stays home, the watchman patrols faster, the festival empties out. Combat in a thunderstorm is not combat on a sunny afternoon. *Everything talks to everything.*

### Terrain Rules That Go All the Way Down

> **Geography is tactics. The hill matters.**
>
> Other tools give you "this tile is grass, this tile is stone." Forager gives you elevation, cover, concealment, slope, and traversal cost — and every NPC and combat flow knows how to read them.
>
> The hilltop archer gets the height advantage because she's *actually higher.* The ravine takes a climb check or a long way around because it's *actually a ravine.* The bramble patch costs double movement because it's *actually brambles.* The half-wall gives partial cover because it's *actually half a wall.* The slick rain-soaked cobblestones in the alley impose a balance check because it *just rained.*
>
> Combat positioning becomes a real conversation. Exploration becomes a real puzzle. The rogue scouts ahead and finds the ledge that overlooks the camp. The cleric realizes the cliff face is unscalable in armor. The fighter charges down the slope and gets the bonus because charging downhill is *meant* to be advantage.
>
> Geography isn't paint. It's the table the whole campaign sits on.

### NPCs With Memory

> **They remember.**
>
> Every NPC in Forager carries a memory log. Each one tracks what's happened to them and what they've witnessed. The smith remembers you bought the fine longsword last spring. The barkeep remembers you started the brawl. The merchant your party robbed in Chapter Two? Three chapters later, in a different city, his brother knows your faces.
>
> Memory propagates. Significant events spread between NPCs who would plausibly talk to each other. Gossip is *a system.* The crime you committed in the harbor district reaches the inquisitor's office in two game days if the witness is connected enough. The favor you did the smith's daughter reaches her cousin, the captain of the watch, by the end of the week.
>
> The world keeps score. You don't have to track it. The simulation tracks it for you, and the NPCs surface it when the moment is right.

### The Inspector — Click Anything, See Everything

> **The simulation is not a black box.**
>
> Click an NPC: see their full schedule, current goal, disposition toward each party member, recent memory entries, and current behavior state. Click a building: see who's inside, what's stored, what's been written, what's been damaged. Click a tile: see terrain type, elevation, movement cost, cover value, current weather effect, and any active triggers.
>
> Depth without legibility is chaos. Forager is deep *and* readable. Every system the simulation runs is one click away from being on screen. Your players can understand why an NPC fled. You can understand why the patrol's behavior changed. The world isn't magic — it's machinery, and the machinery is *visible.*

### Emergent Storytelling — The Stories You Didn't Write

> **The best moments at your table will not be the ones you planned.**
>
> Here's what Forager actually does that no one else does: it lets the systems collide.
>
> The baker is walking to market when the rain starts. She doesn't have a cloak — her schedule didn't account for storms. She ducks under the eaves of the inn the party is drinking in. The fighter, drunk, makes a comment. Disposition shift. Two sessions later, the party needs a favor from the bakery. The baker remembers. *Nobody scripted that.* It happened because rain hit a schedule that hit a player that hit a memory log.
>
> The bandits camped in the forest patrol at night. The lookout on the eastern ridge has high ground and the wind is at his back, so he hears the party coming before they're in sight. He alerts the camp. The party walks into an awake, prepared enemy instead of the sleeping one they expected. *Nobody scripted that.* It happened because weather hit terrain hit AI hit player position.
>
> Forager doesn't write your stories. It creates the conditions for stories to write themselves. Plug in the variables. Hit play. Watch what emerges. *This is the part that makes long-time GMs tear up a little when they see it work.*

### Time-Of-Day Behavior

> **The town at noon is not the town at midnight.**
>
> Markets fill in the morning and empty in the afternoon. Taverns crowd at dusk. The watch changes shifts at dawn and sundown. Children play in the square between three and five. Drunkards stumble home at eleven.
>
> All of it emerges from individual NPC schedules running in parallel. You don't author crowd behavior. You author people, and the crowd is the byproduct. Walk through Margery's village at 7am and it feels like a morning. Walk through at 11pm and it feels like a night. Because it *is.*

### Ambient Soundscape

> **Footsteps on cobblestones. Wind in the trees. A door creaking on its hinges.**
>
> Every terrain type has a footfall sound. Every room has an ambient layer. Every door has a sound when it opens. Every torch has the soft hiss of flame. The whole world is *audible* — and the sound responds to weather, time of day, and what the player is doing.
>
> Pulled directly from the same library system Guildstew already ships, so it composes with the music and SFX you already use at the table.

### Vehicles and Mounts

> **Saddle a horse. Climb on a wagon. Board a ship.**
>
> Mounts increase speed and reduce stamina drain across long distances. Wagons carry party members and gear. Boats let you cross water where you'd otherwise have to swim. Ships have decks you can paint as their own scenes — the party walks below deck and Forager treats it like any other interior.
>
> Travel becomes its own gameplay layer. The horseback chase across a moor. The cart that breaks an axle in the storm. The ship's hold where something is moving. All of it modeled. All of it ready to play.

### Tracking and Investigation

> **Mud tells you who was here.**
>
> Footprints persist. NPCs that walked through wet ground leave readable traces. The party's tracker can examine a path, roll a check, and learn how many came through, when, and where they went. Mystery campaigns get a *physics-driven evidence system* for free.
>
> Combined with the memory system and trigger events, this turns Forager into a competent engine for investigation games as well as combat-forward ones. Stalking the killer through the snow is no longer flavor text. It's gameplay.

## More "Imagine This" Scenarios

### Scene 6 — The Trap That Waited

> A GM, six weeks ago, painted an invisible trigger over the bridge to the old monastery. *If the cursed amulet is in the party's inventory AND it's after sundown.* They forgot about it. The campaign moved on. Tonight, six sessions later, the party finally has the amulet and they're crossing the bridge after dark. The trigger fires. The bridge-keeper rises. Nobody at the table — not even the GM — saw it coming. They look at each other across the screen and someone whispers *holy shit.*

### Scene 7 — The Memory

> Two campaigns ago, in a village the party has not returned to since, the rogue cheated a horse trader. The trader is dead now, killed in a raid. But his apprentice survived. The apprentice has moved to the capital. The party arrives in the capital. They go to buy supplies. The apprentice is the stablemaster. He looks up. *He remembers their faces.* He charges them triple. Nobody scripted that. The memory log surfaced it.

### Scene 8 — Weather Changes Everything

> The party plans a careful ambush on the patrol road. Crossbows on the ridge. Mage in the trees. Quiet positioning, days of prep. Storm rolls in at dusk. Wind shifts. The rain hammers their cover. One of the crossbows fouls. The patrol they were ambushing has a tracker who reads the mud they left getting into position. The ambush becomes a fight on terrain they didn't choose against an enemy that knew they were coming. *None of this was in the GM's prep notes.* The systems made it.

### Scene 9 — The Inspector Reveal

> A player clicks on the village watchman during a quiet moment. The inspector opens. They see his full schedule, his disposition toward each party member, a memory log entry that says he saw the party's bard kissing the mayor's daughter behind the apothecary three nights ago. The bard looks horrified. The watchman has not said a word about it. He's been waiting. *The simulation tracked the consequence the GM didn't even realize was loaded.*

### Scene 10 — Tracks in the Snow

> The party returns to a remote outpost. Heavy snow overnight. The tracker rolls perception. The snow shows: two pairs of boots leaving the outpost, walking east, recent. One pair returning, walking back, hours later. Someone left. Someone didn't come home. The party gets on the trail. The story *the GM didn't have to write* unfolds because the world wrote it.
